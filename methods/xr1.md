 1107: A. Implementation Details
 1108: In this section, we provide a detailed description of the XR-1 framework, focusing on the architecture and training of the
 1109: dual-branch VQ-VAE. The model is designed to encode both vision dynamics and robotic motion into a shared discrete
 1110: latent space, thereby enabling seamless integration of perception and control.
 1111: 
 1112: A.1. Dual-Branch VQ-VAE
 1113: To achieve a unified latent representation, we introduce a dual-branch VQ-VAE consisting of two complementary encoders,
 1114: a vision encoder and a motion encoder, that map their respective modalities into a common discrete codebook. Each
 1115: branch is paired with a decoder to facilitate reconstruction during pretraining. The overall design ensures that the majority
 1116: of representational capacity resides in the encoders, while the decoders primarily serve as auxiliary components for
 1117: reconstruction.
 1118: 
 1119: Vision Branch. The vision branch processes raw image observations {ot , ot+h } and encodes them into compact latent
 1120: tokens.
 1121: Vision Branch Encoder. We adopt SigLIP (Zhai et al., 2023) as the backbone vision encoder, comprising approximately
 1122: 400M parameters. This encoder extracts high-level features from visual inputs. To capture temporal dynamics beyond static
 1123: representations, we incorporate a visual dynamic module inspired by (Chen et al., 2025). This module is implemented as a
 1124: four-layer transformer (ViT (He et al., 2022)) with 32M parameters, which compresses vision dynamic information into a
 1125: fixed number of latent tokens by querying dynamic features.
 1126: Vision Branch Decoder. For reconstruction, we employ a ViT-based decoder with 12 transformer layers (94M parameters).
 1127: Importantly, the encoder contains roughly five times more parameters than the decoder. This asymmetry is intentional: by
 1128: allocating more capacity to encoding, we encourage the model to produce informative latent tokens that simplify downstream
 1129: decoding. Consequently, the decoder remains lightweight since its role is auxiliary rather than representationally dominant.
 1130: During training, all parameters in both the SigLIP backbone and the dynamic module remain fully trainable. Additional
 1131: details regarding training hyperparameters are provided in Table 5.
 1132: 
 1133: Motion Branch.     The motion branch encodes action sequences {at:t+h } into discrete motion codes.
 1134: Motion Branch Encoder. To capture temporal dependencies across actions, we employ 1D causal strided convolutions (Van
 1135: Den Oord et al., 2016), which progressively reduce sequence length h while preserving causality. The stride configuration
 1136: determines the degree of temporal abstraction achieved at each stage. Following this convolutional compression, an 8-layer
 1137: transformer encoder (34M parameters) further contextualizes action embeddings before quantization into discrete tokens.
 1138: Motion Branch Decoder. For action reconstruction, we leverage Gemma (Beyer et al., 2024), an autoregressive language
 1139: model with approximately 300M parameters. The design closely follows the action expert structure in π0 (Black et al., 2024),
 1140: integrating diffusion-based supervision for reconstructing low-level actions from motion codes. Pretraining this decoder
 1141: equips it with strong generative priors over action sequences, thereby providing an effective initialization for downstream
 1142: policy learning. Additional details regarding training hyperparameters are provided in Table 5
 1143: Overall, this dual-branch architecture ensures that both perception and motion are represented in a unified tokenized space
 1144: via vector quantization (VQ), enabling scalable pretraining across multimodal data sources.

 1203: the backbone within the SwitchVLA framework (Li et al., 2025a). This substitution enables faster training and inference
 1204: while lowering memory requirements, making XR-1-Light more suitable for resource-constrained scenarios.

 1206: the supervisory signal UVMC from the Dual-Branch VQ-VAE, which encodes both robotic motion and visual dynamics.
 1207: To integrate this supervision effectively, we employ two learnable tokens, [ZM O] and [ZV IS], that are responsible for
 1208: predicting the motion codes and the visual dynamics codes. Unlike in XR-1 where these tokens are attached to a decoder-only
 1209: transformer backbone, in Florence-2 they are inserted between the encoder and decoder layers. This design allows the
 1210: encoder to specialize in extracting structured latent representations aligned with UVMC, while enabling the decoder to

 1213: on XR-D before fine-tuning on downstream tasks, XR-1-Light omits this stage due to its lightweight architecture. Instead,

 1238: A.3. Training and Inference
 1239: The training of our framework is organized into three stages: UVMC learning, UVMC-guided pretraining, and policy
 1240: fine-tuning. Each stage progressively aligns perception, representation, and control while balancing computational efficiency.
 1241: In the first stage, the UVMC module, containing approximately 0.9B parameters, is pretrained on large-scale multimodal
 1242: data. This process consumed roughly 38,400 GPU hours on a cluster of 80 NVIDIA A100 GPUs (80GB each), enabling the
 1243: model to capture both motion and visual dynamics representations.
 1244: The second stage involves policy pretraining, where the complete model scales up to about 4B parameters. This step also
 1245: required around 38,400 GPU hours on the same hardware configuration. The objective here is to integrate the pretrained
 1246: UVMC representations into a unified vision-language-action policy.
 1247: In the final stage, policy fine-tuning is performed for embodiment-specific adaptation. Each embodiment configuration
 1248: is fine-tuned across 20 downstream tasks using 8 A100 GPUs (80GB), requiring approximately 576 GPU hours per
 1249: embodiment. This ensures that XR-1 and its variants generalize effectively to diverse robotic environments while remaining
 1250: computationally practical.
 1251: For inference, we emphasize both responsiveness and throughput. The system operates with an action chunk inference
 1252: frequency of about 5 Hz while maintaining an average action-level inference rate close to 200 Hz (actions per second).
 1253: These frequencies are achieved on a single commercially available RTX 4090 GPU (24GB), demonstrating that despite
 1254: large-scale pretraining costs, deployment remains efficient without reliance on massive compute resources.

 1917: H. Failure Case Analysis on Baseline Methods
 1918: We conducted a qualitative analysis of the rollout videos to investigate why baselines struggle compared to XR-1. We
 1919: categorize the observed failures into two primary modes:
 1920: Optimization Collapse and Conflicting Gradients. In some distinct scenarios, baselines fail to capture the correct motion
 1921: trend entirely. The models exhibit ”hesitation” or revert to the mean pose, suggesting that the optimization objective is torn
 1922: between conflicting gradients from different tasks. Example: In the ‘DFR-CloseToolbox‘ task, the π0 policy initiates a
 1923: downward movement with the right arm but immediately retracts to the initial position. The robot appears indecisive and
 1924: fails to commit to the task trajectory. We attribute this to the difficulty of fitting a single policy distribution to 20 diverse
 1925: tasks without task-distinguishing representations.
