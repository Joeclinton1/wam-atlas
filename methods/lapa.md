  216: 3.1   L ATENT ACTION Q UANTIZATION
  217: 
  218: To learn latent actions in a fully unsupervised manner, we train a latent action quantization model
  219: following Bruce et al. (2024) with a few modifications. Our latent action quantization model is
  220: an encoder-decoder architecture where the encoder takes the current frame xt and the future frame
  221: xt+H of a video with a fixed window size H and outputs the latent action zt 1 . The decoder is
  222: trained to take the latent action zt and xt and reconstruct xt+H . Unlike Bruce et al. (2024), we
  223: use cross attention to attend zt given xt instead of additive embedding, which empirically leads
  224: to capturing more semantically meaningful latent actions. Our quantization model is a variant of
  225: C-ViViT tokenizer (Villegas et al., 2023) where the encoder includes both spatial and temporal
  226: transformer while the decoder only contains spatial transformer since our model uses only two image
  227: frames as input.
  228: Our latent action quantization training model is based on the VQ-VAE objective (van den Oord et al.,
  229: 2017), where the nearest quantized representation from the continuous embedding is retrieved from
  230: an embedding space where each embedding corresponds to a codebook. The VQ-VAE objective
  231: enables the latent action zt to be discrete tokens (codebooks), making it easy for VLMs to predict
  232: zt . The latent action is represented using s sequences from |C| codebook vocabulary space. The
  233: sequence length s is designated by the kernel size, stride and padding value of a CNN network which
  234: is used right before the vector quantization process. To avoid gradient collapse often observed
  235: in VQ-VAE, we utilize NSVQ (Vali & Bäckström, 2022) which replaces the vector quantization
  236: error to a product of original error and a normalized noise vector. We also apply stop gradient to
  237: the patch embedding of the frame xt during decoding to avoid representation collapse. Codebook
  238: replacement technique from NSVQ is applied during early training steps to maximize codebook
  239: utilization. Further model and training details are provided in Appendix A.
  240: We utilize the encoder of our latent action quantization model as an inverse dynamics model in la-
  241: tent pretraining and the decoder for generating neural-based closed-loop rollouts. Unlike previous
  242: works (Bruce et al., 2024; Valevski et al., 2024), our approach trains both a world model that gen-
  243: erates rollouts from the latent actions and a policy model that produces these latent actions through
  244: Latent Pretraining.

  247: 3.2   L ATENT P RETRAINING
  248: 
  249: We use the encoder of the latent action quantization model as an inverse dynamics model to label
  250: all frames xt , given frame xt+1 , with latent action zt . Then, we do action pretraining by using
  251: a pretrained VLM to predict the zt given the language instruction of a video clip and the current
  252: image xt . Instead of using the existing language model head of the VLM, we attach a separate
  253: latent action head (a single MLP layer) of vocab size |C|. By default, we freeze only the vision
  254: encoder and unfreeze the language model during training. Since latent pretraining does not rely on
  255: ground truth actions, it opens the possibility of using any type of raw video paired with language
  256: instructions. Also, in contrast to traditional action granularity used in robotics (e.g. end-effector
  257: positions, joint positions, joint torques, etc.), our approach does not require any priors about the
  258: action hierarchy/granularity and is learned in an end-to-end manner simply by being optimized to
  259: best capture the ‘delta’ of consecutive observations in a given video dataset. We broadly refer to
  260: models having gone through latent pretraining as LAPA.

  698: downstream tasks compared to existing approaches. We also present a state-of-the-art VLA model

  706: a smaller head predicts actions at a higher frequency. Lastly, while we qualitatively demonstrate
