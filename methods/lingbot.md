  281: video-action world modeling framework that jointly models visual observations and robot actions within a single
  282: autoregressive process. Unlike prior approaches that either decouple video prediction from action inference [16, 27]
  283: or rely on bidirectional diffusion within segments [97], our method unifies video and action within a single causal
  284: autoregressive framework, enabling persistent memory through KV cache and seamless integration of real-time
  285: observations.
  286: World Dynamics with Autoregressive Modeling. Recent world models for robotics often adopt bidirectional video
  287: generation approaches [4, 20, 24, 42] or learn interactive simulators [86], which face fundamental limitations for
  288: closed-loop control. Open-loop methods that generate entire long sequences in one shot incur prohibitive computational
  289: cost and cannot incorporate real-time feedback for error correction. Chunk-based diffusion methods that generate video
  290: segments sequentially [22, 97] suffer from two critical issues: (1) they lack persistent memory across chunks, as each
  291: chunk is generated independently without access to the full history, leading to temporal inconsistencies and drift over
  292: long horizons; (2) the bidirectional attention within each chunk violates causality, preventing seamless integration with
  293: real-time observations during execution.
  294:    The physical world, however, is inherently causal and autoregressive: the present state depends only on the past, and
  295: we cannot observe the future before it occurs. This fundamental property motivates our autoregressive world modeling
  296: approach, which offers three critical advantages over chunk-based diffusion for robotic control: (1) Persistent Memory:
  297: by explicitly conditioning on the complete observation history through causal attention and KV cache, the model
  298: maintains long-term context and temporal coherence across the entire trajectory, avoiding the “amnesia” problem of
  299: chunk-based methods; (2) Causal Consistency: the unidirectional dependency structure naturally aligns with closed-loop
  300: execution, where new observations can be seamlessly incorporated as they arrive; (3) Efficiency: chunk-wise prediction
  301: with parallel generation within each chunk balances computational efficiency with autoregressive flexibility, enabling
  302: high-frequency control with real-time error correction.

  345: 3.3     LingBot-VA: Unified Architecture & Training
  346: Architecture. To jointly model video and action generation, we leverage a dual-stream diffusion transformer architecture
  347: that performs conditional flow matching for autoregressive prediction. Our model consists of two parallel transformer
  348: backbones: a video stream initialized from Wan2.2-5B (a large-scale pretrained video generation model with dimension
  349: dv [79]), and an action stream with same depth but significantly smaller width da ≪ dv . This asymmetric design is
  350: motivated by the observation that action distributions are inherently simpler than visual data requiring fewer parameters
  351: to model effectively while maintaining expressive capacity for visual dynamics.
  352: Video Sparsification. Video frames exhibit significant temporal redundancy, especially in robotic manipulation where
  353: scenes evolve gradually. We sparsify the video sequence by temporally downsampling frames by a factor of τ = 4,
  354: reducing visual tokens while improving efficiency [5]. Since actions evolve at higher frequency than visual changes,
  355: we interleave the downsampled video tokens with action tokens in temporal order: for each video frame ot , we
  356: associate τ consecutive actions {at,1 , at,2 , . . . , at,τ }, forming a unified sequence [zt , at,1 , at,2 , . . . , at,τ , zt+1 , . . .] for
  357: joint modeling. This design means that predicting K video frames corresponds to generating τ K actions, enabling
  358: high-frequency control while maintaining efficient video generation.

 1066: 5                Related Work
 1067: Vision-Language-Action Policies. Recent advancements in Embodied AI have witnessed a paradigm shift toward large-
 1068: scale Vision-Language-Action (VLA) policies. By leveraging web-scale knowledge and diverse robot demonstrations,
 1069: models such as π0.5 [29], GR-3 [39], and GR00T-N1 [6] achieve remarkable generalizability across various manipulation
 1070: tasks without relying on hand-crafted rules, modular priors, or restricted action abstractions, enabling a more direct
 1071: and expressive end-to-end mapping from perception to control. These policies typically employ pre-trained Vision-
 1072: Language Models (VLMs) as foundational backbones [6, 7, 11, 29, 34, 39, 87, 93], which provide superior cross-modal
 1073: understanding and more generalizable action distributions compared to task-specific imitation policies like ACT [91] or
 1074: Diffusion Policy [17]. Efforts have been further devoted to improving the deployability through lightweight backbones
 1075: [49, 62, 67], efficient tokenization [57], real-time inference [8, 10, 70], or fine-tuning schemes [30, 32, 38]. However,
 1076: despite their prowess in semantic reasoning, a fundamental limitation persists: the pre-training objectives and data
 1077: distributions of standard VLMs largely overlook the fine-grained system dynamics and low-level trajectories essential
 1078: for precision manipulation. While supervised fine-tuning on expensively collected large-scale robot datasets allows these
 1079: models to approximate the marginal action distribution [3, 31, 53], they remain deficient in capturing the underlying
 1080: transition dynamics—specifically, how the physical state of the environment should evolve and will evolve. Furthermore,
 1081: most current VLA methods formulate control as a purely reactive mapping from instantaneous observations to actions.
 1082: This approach inherently fails to account for the historical context necessary to resolve ambiguities in non-Markovian
 1083: environments. Additionally, the static image-text pre-training inherent in VLMs fails to instill essential temporal priors.
 1084: Even when augmented with memory modules [37, 65, 68], such models remain unable to reason about the causal and
 1085: sequential nature of physical interactions. To address these shortcomings, recent research has pivoted towards generalist
 1086: robot policies grounded in world models and generative video modeling [1, 5, 40, 64, 97]. However, these methods
 1087: typically generate predictions with bidirectional attention, which violates the causal structure of physical dynamics and
 1088: lacks persistent long-term memory across the full execution history. Our LingBot-VA unifies autoregressive video

 1093: past observations and actions. By maintaining a persistent KV cache over the complete interaction history, LingBot-VA

 1095: visual evolution of the environment.
 1096: World Models for Robotic Control. Inspired by human reliance on intuitive physics to anticipate environmental
 1097: changes, world models aim to facilitate effective planning by predicting future dynamics. Existing approaches are
 1098: generally categorized into three groups based on their state representations. The first category operates in latent
 1099: space [36, 41, 63, 80], encoding task-relevant features into compact vectors to predict evolution via probabilistic
 1100: [36, 52, 83] or deterministic methods [63, 85]. The second category utilizes 3D point clouds [66, 69, 77], leveraging
 1101: Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to predict geometric evolution [88, 89], which is particularly effective for manipulating
 1102: deformable objects [77, 89]. The third category focuses on 2D pixel space, directly predicting future keyframes or
 1103: video sequences [21, 33, 95, 96]. Our work aligns with this third category. Within this domain, approaches range from
 1104: co-training with video generation for representation learning [14, 40, 97] to serving as simulators for policy learning or
 1105: evaluation [71]. Our research specifically targets methods that predict future frames during execution to condition action
 1106: generation. However, prior video-conditioned methods predominantly rely on open-loop generation [21, 95], presenting
 1107: two significant challenges. First, the misalignment between generated videos and real-world dynamics, coupled with
 1108: cumulative drift from execution errors, often leads to suboptimal performance. Second, the computational intensity of
 1109: video generation imposes high latency, severely hindering real-time inference. Our method leverages KV Cache and
 1110: causal masking to continuously update the model’s memory with real-world observations. This effectively transitions
 1111: the system to a closed-loop control mechanism, mitigating error accumulation in long-horizon tasks. Furthermore, we
 1112: introduce a partial denoising strategy, enabling action generation from intermediate representations without waiting for
 1113: fully denoised frames.
