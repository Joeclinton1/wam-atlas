   77: 1. Introduction
   78: Recent advances in Vision–Language–Action (VLA) models have enabled generalist robot control through
   79: large-scale multimodal alignment [3, 17, 49]. By embedding visual observations and language instructions
   80: into a shared semantic latent space, these models can generalize across diverse manipulation tasks and
   81: environments [9, 30]. However, while vision supports high-level semantic understanding and language
   82: specifies task intent, physical interaction is fundamentally governed by tactile feedback, the only modality that
   83: directly encodes instantaneous contact dynamics between the robot and its environment. Tactile sensing
   84: is particularly critical for fine-grained, contact-rich manipulation, such as handling fragile, deformable, or
   85: slippery objects. Unlike vision, which captures relatively stable object geometry from a distance, tactile
   86: signals reflect the transient spatiotemporal evolution of forces at the contact interface. Effective use of this
   87: modality requires not only spatial reasoning over force distributions but also temporal reasoning over how
   88: these distributions evolve under dynamic interaction.
   89: Most existing tactile-augmented VLA architectures incorporate tactile information by either (1) projecting
   90: tactile embeddings into a pre-trained vision–language latent space, treating them as additional semantic
   91: tokens [37], or (2) concatenating tactile features with language-conditioned visual representations in the
   92: downstream policy [2, 11, 16]. While these approaches expose the model to tactile inputs, they place a
   93: substantial burden on representation learning: the model must implicitly infer contact physics within a
   94: semantic embedding space optimized for visual alignment and static scene description rather than physical
   95: prediction. Learning that particular tactile patterns correspond to slip, deformation, or instability requires
   96: discovering these concepts indirectly through static correlations. This often demands large-scale annotated
   97: data and offers no guarantee that the underlying high-frequency dynamics will be faithfully captured. Without
   98: explicit temporal modeling, these learned representations struggle to encode the causal relationships between
   99: successive tactile frames, precisely the structure needed to anticipate failure modes such as incipient slip
  100: [22, 32, 42]. Moreover, because many VLA backbones prioritize semantic alignment over predictive physical
  101: modeling, they further underutilize tactile signals for fine-grained spatial and temporal reasoning [2, 16].
  102: We address these limitations by introducing VTAM, a generalist Video–Tactile Action Model that integrates
  103: tactile sensing into a predictive world-model framework for contact-rich manipulation, as shown in Fig. 1.
  104: At the representation level, we design a visuo–tactile predictive module built on top of a pretrained video
  105: backbone. Instead of mapping tactile signals into a language-aligned semantic space, VTAM treats touch as a
  106: primary sensory modality and jointly predicts the future evolution of visual and tactile streams conditioned on
  107: the robot’s end-effector state. This predictive formulation enables the backbone to learn temporally consistent

  119: • We introduce VTAM, a visuo–tactile world action model that integrates high-resolution tactile sensing with
  120:   visual observations within a predictive video backbone to enable robust contact-rich robotic manipulation.

  136: 2. Related Works
  137: Vision-Language-Action Models. VLA models have emerged as the dominant paradigm for generalist
  138: robot control, leveraging internet-scale vision–language pretraining to ground natural-language instructions
  139: in visual observations and decode motor commands through a unified architecture [3, 4, 17, 30, 49].
  140: Subsequent efforts have expanded the paradigm along several axes, incorporating 3D geometric priors [28,
  141: 46], hierarchical task planning [1, 23], and predictive world knowledge [44, 47], consistently improving
  142: generalization and sample efficiency. Existing visuo-linguistic VLAs struggle with physical interactions
  143: when visual cues are occluded, particularly with fragile objects. VTAM targets this gap by incorporating
  144: high-resolution tactile observations directly into a generative world-model backbone: the model learns joint
  145: visuo–tactile dynamics and uses these representations to guide action generation, so tactile cues can correct
  146: visual misestimation during interaction and improve robustness on fragile and force-sensitive tasks.
  147: Generative World Models for Robotics. Generative world models forecast future environment states to
  148: support planning and policy learning [26, 38]. Recent work has scaled this idea by jointly diffusing video
  149: and action trajectories [19]. DreamZero [39] builds a World Action Model on a pretrained video diffusion
  150: backbone, achieving zero-shot generalization and cross-embodiment transfer by learning physical dynamics
  151: from heterogeneous robot data. UWM [47] introduces modality-specific diffusion timesteps that decouple
  152: video and action noise schedules, enabling pretraining on large-scale datasets that include action-free
  153: video. DreamVLA [44] augments VLAs with future visual token prediction, and RDP [36] applies diffusion
  154: hierarchically for contact-aware action refinement.
  155: Despite these advances, most existing world models encode environmental dynamics almost exclusively
  156: through visual prediction. While visual forecasting captures object motion and scene evolution, it provides only
  157: indirect access to the physical interaction signals that govern contact-rich manipulation. Critical phenomena
  158: such as slip, deformation, and force transfer arise at the contact interface and are often weakly observable or
  159: entirely hidden from camera views. As a result, models that rely solely on visual dynamics may struggle to
  160: anticipate failure modes during delicate or force-sensitive interactions. Motivated by this limitation, VTAM
  161: brings tactile deformation dynamics into the predictive world model and anchors control learning with a
  162: virtual-force target so the policy remains responsive when contact becomes visually ambiguous.

  466: 4.4. Qualitative Examples and Failure Mode Analysis
  467: 
  468: Figure 4 shows qualitative comparisons across the three tasks. We analyze the behaviors of different methods
  469: to understand how VTAM addresses the challenges in contact-rich tasks. Additional examples can be found
  470: in the Appendix.
  471: Chip Pick-and-Place. For the GE vision-only baseline, the main failure arises from the inability to verify
  472: successful grasps. The robot often closes the gripper above the chip and proceeds to the plate even when the
  473: grasp fails. The Pi variants with and without tactile input exhibit similar behaviors, indicating that methods
  474: without effective tactile signal integration cannot perform correct force-aware grasping. In contrast, VTAM
  475: exhibits tactile-aware behaviors. The robot lifts the chip only when tactile deformation confirms successful
  476: contact and maintains a stable gripper width to prevent dropping during lifting. In case the grasp fails, the
  477: policy can also detect the absence of tactile signals during lifting and immediately return to the chip to
  478: reattempt the grasp, rather than continuing to the plate and releasing the gripper.
  479: Cucumber Peeling. The GE baseline and both Pi variants exhibit similar motion patterns. Starting from
  480: the left side of the cucumber, the tool first moves toward the centerline and then moves away from it while
  481: sliding along the surface. This trajectory resembles a vision-driven strategy that attempts to follow the
  482: object curvature rather than regulating contact force. As a result, the tool frequently loses contact with
  483: the cucumber surface. In contrast, our VTAM policy establishes stable contact and maintains proper force
  484: while moving along the surface. The robot can perform repeated peeling motions at the same location,
  485: demonstrating accurate perception of contact states even when the cucumber thickness varies.
  486: Whiteboard Wiping. On both flat and tilted whiteboards, the two Pi variants apply excessively large contact
  487: forces, sometimes even pushing the books used to support the tilted board out of place. This behavior
  488: likely arises because the training data contains both flat and tilted surfaces, making it difficult for the
  489: model to infer the correct end-effector height from visual observations alone. As a result, the policy cannot
  490: reliably determine when the gripper should move downward to follow a lower surface or remain higher to
  491: accommodate a tilted plane, and instead compensates by applying excessive force to maintain contact.
  492: In terms of the GE baseline, although it occasionally performs light wiping motions on flat surfaces, the contact
  493: becomes unstable and the end-effector motion turns irregular on tilted boards. The policy tends to follow a
  494: trajectory suitable for flat surfaces, causing the end-effector to press excessively against higher regions of the
  495: tilted board while failing to maintain stable contact when the surface height changes. In contrast, VTAM
  496: maintains moderate and stable contact forces on both flat and tilted surfaces, enabling consistent wiping
  497: and effective stain removal. This reflects that VTAM can effectively leverage tactile information to handle
  498: visually ambiguous contact-rich tasks.

  561: 5. Conclusion
  562: We introduce VTAM, a visuo–tactile world action model for contact-rich manipulation. VTAM trains a
  563: predictive backbone to model the joint evolution of multi-view video and high-resolution tactile signals,
  564: so the policy can use contact dynamics that are weakly observable or occluded in vision. This predictive
  565: formulation learns temporally consistent contact features without requiring explicit labels for contact events
  566: and avoids relying on purely downstream, reactive tactile fusion. To prevent the common failure mode where
  567: action training defaults to visual cues and suppresses tactile information, we add a deformation-derived
  568: virtual-force prediction objective that maintains tactile supervision through the control pathway. On real
  569: robot tasks that demand precise force regulation, including chip pick-and-place and cucumber peeling, VTAM
  570: substantially improves success rate and interaction stability over vision-only and naive tactile baselines,
  571: highlighting the importance of modeling contact dynamics directly for reliable physical interaction. Ultimately,
  572: our framework provides a scalable, physically grounded approach to embodied intelligence, proving that
  573: predictive joint modeling is essential for reliable execution in complex physical interactions.
