  548: Table 1 shows that ViPRA achieves the highest average success rate across both discrete and con-

  553: 20.8%, π0 by 35.4%, and UniVLA by 19.8%. This suggests that motion-centric latent pretraining
  554: combined with joint future-state modeling provides stronger control priors than both training from
  555: scratch and temporally coarse latent approaches. Although ViPRA-AR slightly exceeds ViPRA-FM
  556: in the low-noise simulation regime, likely due to faster convergence, ViPRA-FM remains compet-
  557: itive and surpasses all other continuous and discrete baselines. ViPRA also surpasses other video
  558: learning, i.e., UniPI and VPT. UniPI frequently generates action sequences that diverge from the
  559: given instruction in longer-horizon settings, while VPT provides only limited gains, indicating that
  560: IDM-derived pseudo-labels are sensitive to environment shifts. In contrast, ViPRA’s joint use of la-
  561: tent action prediction and future state modeling yields stronger cross-environment transfer and more
  562: reliable task execution.

  722: 6   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
  723: 
  724: ViPRA shows that motion-centric latent actions learned from large-scale actionless videos can form
  725: a strong foundation for generalist robot policies. Combined with video-language pretraining and
  726: flow matching adaptation, this enables smooth, high-frequency control with limited labeled data.
  727: While effective across simulation and real robots, several limitations remain and point to important
  728: directions for future research.
  729: Embodiment and dexterity. Our approach generalizes across embodiments such as WidowX and
  730: Franka, including bimanual setups, but extending to more dexterous domains like dual-arm hu-
  731: manoids or multi-fingered hands introduces new challenges. These platforms demand finer latent
  732: actions capable of representing precise contact dynamics and inter-arm coordination. Incorporat-
  733: ing tactile or force feedback, or learning embodiment-specific adapters, may allow latent actions to
  734: better capture such motion primitives.
  735: Data scope and generalization. Although ViPRA leverages large-scale actionless videos for pre-
  736: training, the diversity of available data remains limited. Expanding to richer ego-centric datasets
  737: with human-hand interactions, tool use, or short navigation episodes could improve embodiment
  738: invariance and temporal grounding. Integrating additional sensing modalities such as wrist cam-
  739: eras, proprioception, tactile feedback, or depth can further strengthen perception-control alignment,
  740: especially in dynamic or unstructured environments.
  741: Scaling behavior and data efficiency. An open question is how passive video pretraining scales
  742: and where diminishing returns emerge. Characterizing scaling behavior and balancing unlabeled
  743: video with smaller quantities of demonstrations would clarify efficient paths for large-scale robot
  744: learning.
  745: Predictive modeling. The latent action decoder can also be interpreted as a predictive world
  746: model. By sampling latent actions, the model can generate visual rollouts, which naturally sup-
  747: port reinforcement-learning-based alignment Bai et al. (2022); Rafailov et al. (2023) and test time
  748: planning with VLM-based reward models (Ma et al., 2024).

 1286: A    BACKGROUND
 1287: We review key paradigms relevant for ViPRA: VQ-VAE for imposing information bottleneck to
 1288: learn discrete latent actions, optical flow estimation, behavior cloning for direct action prediction
 1289: and flow matching for smooth continuous control.
 1290: Vector-Quantized VAEs (VQ-VAE). An encoder eζ maps an observation ot to a continuous la-
 1291: tent vector h̃t = [h̃1t , . . . , h̃N
 1292:                                     t ], which is quantized to a sequence of nearest codewords zt =
 1293: [zt1 , . . . , ztN ] ∈ C using a shared discrete codebook. A decoder dζ reconstructs the observation
 1294: ôt = dψ (zt + Err), where Err is a gradient estimator used to allow backpropagation through the
 1295: non-differentiable quantization operation.
 1296: In traditional VQ-VAE (van den Oord et al., 2017), this estimator takes the form
 1297:                                           ErrSTE = sg(h̃t − zt ),                                       (4)
 1298: where sg(·) denotes the stop-gradient operator. The decoder input zt + ErrSTE preserves the forward
 1299: pass while enabling gradients to bypass the non-differentiable argmin. However, this approach
 1300: typically requires auxiliary losses, such as the codebook and commitment losses, to stabilize training
 1301: and encourage codebook usage.We adopt the NSVQ formulation (Vali & Bäckström, 2022), which
 1302: replaces the deterministic STE with a stochastic noise-injected surrogate
 1303:                                          ErrNSVQ = ∥zt − h̃t ∥ · ϵ̃                                     (5)
 1304: where ϵ̃ = ϵ/∥ϵ∥ and ϵ ∼ N (0, I). The decoder thus receives ôt = dψ (zt + ErrNSVQ ). Crucially,
 1305: NSVQ enables gradients to flow to both the encoder and codebook using only the reconstruction
 1306: loss, without requiring additional codebook loss terms. The noise-injected gradient estimator, com-
 1307: bined with the unused codebook replacement technique applied during early training, significantly
 1308: improves training stability and mitigates codebook collapse, a common issue in VQ-VAE training.
 1309: RAFT based Optical Flow Given two images, oa and ob , RAFT (Teed & Deng, 2020) obtains the
 1310: dense displacement field fa→b ∈ RH×W ×2 that maps each pixel in frame oa to its location in ob . It
 1311:                                                ′   ′
 1312: first extracts feature maps ϕ(oa ), ϕ(ob ) ∈ RH ×W ×d (where ϕ is the feature extractor) and builds
 1313: the all-pair correlation R, with Rij,kl = ⟨ϕij (oa ), ϕkl (ob )⟩. The flow prediction is then refined
 1314: iteratively: f (k+1) = f (k) + ∆f (k) (R). When applied to temporally close frames in a video, this
 1315: flow field f can give a good estimate of motion consistency.
 1316: Behavior Cloning (BC) is a supervised learning paradigm in robotics that learns policies directly
 1317: from expert demonstrations. Given a dataset D = {(ot , at )}Tt=1 of observation-action pairs from
 1318: expert trajectories, BC trains a parameterized policy πBC (at |ot ; θ) to minimize a distance metric
 1319: between predicted and ground-truth actions:
 1320:                                 min E(ot ,at )∼D [d(πBC (at |ot ; θ), at )] ,                           (6)
 1321:                                   θ
 1322: 
 1323: where d(·, ·) is typically the L1 or L2 distance for continuous actions or cross-entropy for discrete
 1324: actions. This framework has been extended with high-capacity architectures: diffusion models (Chi
 1325: et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023) parameterize πBC (at |ot ; θ) as a denoising process that learns p(at |ot )
 1326: through iterative refinement, while VLAs leverage pretrained language models (Touvron et al., 2023;
 1327: Qwen et al., 2024) and visual encoders (Radford et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2023; Tschannen et al.,
 1328: 2025) as the backbone architecture for θ, enabling multimodal grounding of actions in visual and
 1329: linguistic contexts.
 1330: Flow Matching (Lipman et al., 2022) provides an alternative to diffusion models for learning con-
 1331: tinuous normalizing flows. While diffusion models learn the full denoising process, flow matching
 1332: directly learns the vector field that transports samples from a source distribution to a target distri-
 1333: bution. This approach offers computational advantages for robotics applications where real-time
 1334: inference is critical.

 1377: B     L ATENT ACTION L EARNING
 1378: We detail our latent action learning framework in Algorithm 1, which extracts latent action se-
 1379: quences zt:t+L from video sequences ot:t+L using a combination of reconstruction, perceptual, and
 1380: optical flow consistency losses. A detailed diagram of this procedure is shown in Figure 7, and the
 1381: complete training configuration–including model architecture and optimization hyperparameters–is
 1382: provided in Table 5.
 1383: The inverse dynamics encoder maps each frame ok into a sequence of spatial features using a DI-
 1384: NOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023)-initialized backbone. These features are enriched with clip-level context
 1385: through factorized spatio-temporal attention layers, where the temporal branch employs bidirec-
 1386: tional attention to aggregate information across the full sequence. The contextualized features are
 1387: then discretized via Noise-Substitution Vector Quantization (NSVQ) (Vali & Bäckström, 2022), pro-
 1388: ducing Nlatent discrete codes selected from the shared codebook C, which serve as the latent action
 1389: tokens for zk .
 1390: The forward decoder mirrors this architecture with a factorized spatio-temporal transformer, but
 1391: applies causal temporal attention so that predictions depend only on the past. It jointly attends to the
 1392: latent action sequence zt:t+k and the observation history ot:t+k to reconstruct the next frame ot+k+1 .
 1393: In addition, we integrate the action-conditioning modules proposed by (He et al., 2025) before each
 1394: spatio-temporal block in the decoder to better align latent action tokens with visual dynamics.

 1799: We provide a more in-depth analysis of the action outputs of various policies introduced in Sec-

 1802:        • Absolute Joint Space. ViPRA-FM and LAPA (Ye et al., 2024) output full 7D joint posi-

 1804:        • Delta End-Effector Space. OpenVLA (Kim et al., 2024), π0 (Black et al., 2024), and

 1806:          visual inputs.
 1807:        • Continuous vs Discrete. ViPRA-FM and π0 (Black et al., 2024) predict continuous actions
 1808:          via a flow matching decoder, whereas LAPA (Ye et al., 2024) and OpenVLA (Kim et al.,
 1809:          2024) use quantized logits over discretized action bins.
 1810: To better understand the behavioral differences between discrete and continuous policies, we analyze
 1811: the predicted action trajectories across different models during closed-loop visual rollout on real
 1812: robot observations. We evaluate policies by loading their finetuned checkpoints into our inference
 1813: pipeline and simulating replay on the training trajectories from the finetuning dataset. This allows
 1814: us to visualize their motor command trends without introducing new generalization factors. In
 1815: particular, we compare ViPRA-FM (ours), LAPA (Ye et al., 2024), OpenVLA (Kim et al., 2024),
 1816: and π0 (Black et al., 2024).
 1817: LAPA (Ye et al., 2024) and OpenVLA (Kim et al., 2024) rely on a discretization scheme in which
 1818: each dimension of the robot’s action space is uniformly quantized into 255 bins using equal-sized
 1819: quantiles over the training distribution. That is, for each joint or end-effector dimension, bin bound-
 1820: aries are chosen so that each bin contains roughly the same number of training points. This quantile-
 1821: based discretization ensures equal data coverage across bins but introduces two key limitations in
 1822: how actions are represented and learned:
 1823:       1. Contact-Sensitive Flipping: At test time, small perturbations in the input (e.g., due to
 1824:          occlusions or slight viewpoint drift) may cause the model to flip from one bin to another
 1825:          near the quantile boundary–especially at contact points. Since adjacent bins can correspond
 1826:          to different action magnitudes, these minor visual shifts can lead to abrupt discontinuities
 1827:          in motor output.
 1828:       2. Loss Granularity: The cross-entropy loss used for training treats each action bin as a
 1829:          distinct class label. As a result, all incorrect predictions are penalized equally, regardless of
 1830:          how close they are to the ground-truth bin. For example, predicting bin 127 instead of 128
 1831:          incurs the same loss as predicting bin 0. This is fundamentally at odds with the structure of
 1832:          continuous action spaces, where the cost of an error should scale with its magnitude.
 1833: We hypothesize that the combination of bin boundary flipping and non-metric loss leads to the
 1834: spiky or erratic behavior seen in discrete action models, particularly around moments of contact
 1835: or high-frequency motion. These effects are amplified in high-dimensional control settings, where
 1836: discretization artifacts can arise independently in each action dimension–compounding into visibly
 1837: unstable or jerky behaviors across the full joint trajectory.
 1838: By contrast, continuous policies such as ViPRA-FM and π0 (Black et al., 2024) operate directly in
 1839: RD using flow matching. These losses naturally reflect the structure of the action space–penalizing
 1840: predictions in proportion to how far they deviate from the ground truth. As a result, the output
 1841: trajectories tend to be smoother, better aligned with demonstrations, and more robust to perceptual
 1842: jitter.

 1844: bins or by using non-uniform binning schemes (e.g., higher resolution in frequently visited regions).
 1845: However, these approaches increase model complexity and still inherit the fundamental limitation
 1846: of using classification loss in a regression setting. Continuous decoders trained with distance-aware
 1847: objectives offer a more natural and principled solution for low-level control.
