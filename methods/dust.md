 1126: Additional implementation details.
 1127: We base our architecture on the GR00T-N1.5 (NVIDIA

 1129: they pass through an encoder made up of a 3-layer MLP with 2D sinusoidal positional encoding with

 1148: We vary batch size and training time per dataset.
 1149: • For the RoboCasa (Nasiriany et al., 2024) dataset, we train using global batch size 32, with 2
 1150: A100 GPUs. For each training dataset scale, the time until convergence varies, with 100 demos
 1151: requiring 60k steps, 300 demos requiring 420k steps, and 1000 demos requiring 600k steps. The
 1152: long convergence time is mostly due to the small global batch size.
 1153: • For the GR-1 (NVIDIA et al., 2025) dataset, we train using global batch size of 960, with 8 H200
 1154: GPUs over 60k steps. We noted training on GR-1 was very sensitive to batch size and required
 1155: large scale training for meaningful training results.
 1156: • For the real-world dataset, we train using global batch size of 32, with 2 A100 GPUs over 60k
 1157: steps.
 1158: • For the transfer learning setup, we first train with BridgeV2 (Walke et al., 2023) video data using
 1159: global batch size of 32, with 2 A100 GPUs for 120k steps. Then, we finetune using the RoboCasa
 1160: 100 demo dataset with the same GPU setup for 60k steps.
 1161: Common training details.
 1162: Excluding batch size and iteration count, all experiments are done with
 1163: the same training hyperparameters. We optimize with AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) using
 1164: a base learning rate of 1e-4, with β1 = 0.95, β2 = 0.999, and ϵ = 1e-8. Weight decay of 1e-5 is
 1165: applied with the exception of bias and LayerNorm weights. The learning rate follows a cosine decay
 1166: schedule with a 5% warmup period.

 1169: RoboCasa kitchen. RoboCasa is a single arm manipulation benchmark with a focus on kitchen
 1170: environment interaction tasks. We utilize a suite of 24 tasks that span a wide range of common
 1171: household manipulations, including turning sink faucets, closing drawer doors, and moving objects.
 1172: Tasks are categorized into 8 pick-and-place tasks, 6 contraption open/close tasks, and 10 other mis-

 1174: was generated with MimicGen (Mandlekar et al., 2023) within the MuJoCo simulation environment
 1175: (Todorov et al., 2012), with a Franka Emika Panda robot arm serving as the manipulator. Image
 1176: observations include 3 viewpoints from the left, right, and wrist. The robot state/action space is
 1177: parameterized with 7 degrees of freedom (DoF), consisting of end-effector position and rotation
 1178: together with a binary gripper pose. We experiment over 100, 300, and 1000 training episodes per
 1179: task, testing data efficiency and scaling properties.
 1180: GR-1 tabletop tasks. GR-1 is a humanoid robot benchmark with a focus on dexterous tabletop
 1181: manipulation of everyday objects. We utilize a total of 24 tasks consisting of 16 pick-and-place tasks,
 1182: and 8 articulated tasks, the latter adding the requirement of closing containers such as microwaves
 1183: and cabinets after pick-and-place. Training data utilizes data from GR00T-N1.5 (NVIDIA et al.,
 1184: 2025), where the dataset was generated with DexMimicGen (Jiang et al., 2025) in the MuJoCo
 1185: simulation environment (Todorov et al., 2012). The simulated robot is a GR-1 humanoid robot with
 1186: Fourier dexterous hands, enabling fine-grained grasping and manipulation. Image observations are
 1187: taken from a single egocentric view from the robot’s head. The state/action space consists of 29
 1188: DoF in total, 17 DoF corresponding to the GR-1 robot’s arms and waist, and 6 DoF for each of the
 1189: Fourier hands. We experiment over 300 and 1000 training episodes per task.
