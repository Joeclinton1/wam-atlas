  162: 2.2   V IDEO G ENERATION IN ROBOTICS
  163: To overcome the physical blindness of static VLMs, recent research has increasingly turned to gen-
  164: erative video models, which naturally encapsulate rich spatiotemporal priors and complex physical
  165: dynamics (Hu et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2025; Feng et al., 2025; Liao et al., 2025;
  166: Wang et al., 2025; Zhong et al., 2025; Cen et al., 2025; Feng et al., 2025; Bi et al., 2025; Li et al.,
  167: 2026). Historically, video prediction in robotics was primarily utilized for visual foresight, enabling
  168: model-based planning by “imagining” future states (Finn & Levine, 2017; Ebert et al., 2018; Yang
  169: et al., 2023; Du et al., 2023). However, with the advent of high-fidelity diffusion transformers, a
  170: new frontier has emerged that directly integrates video generation into policy learning.
  171: A recent line of work (Shen et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025a; Bi et al., 2025; Li et al., 2026) has ex-
  172: plored projecting both visual dynamics and control signals into a shared latent space. These models
  173: effectively consolidate versatile capabilities (such as forward simulation and inverse dynamics) into
  174: a single learned system. Building upon this trend of explicit unification, Cosmos Policy (Kim et al.,
  175: 2026) further simplifies the adaptation by fine-tuning a pre-trained video diffusion model to directly
  176: output robot actions and future expected values, encoding them as contiguous latent frames within
  177: the native video diffusion process. The most closely related work, mimic-video (Pai et al., 2025),
  178: pairs a pre-trained video backbone with a separate flow-matching action decoder and conditions the
  179: policy on partially denoised video latents at an intermediate flow time. In contrast, we explore joint
  180: training of video and action generation, enabling the action model to learn how to extract effective
  181: features across different stages of the video generation process, yielding more robust representa-
  182: tions.

  469: future frame ôt+1 .

 1244: A.4   L IMITATIONS AND D ISCUSSION
 1245: While DiT4DiT demonstrates exceptional capabilities in bridging generative video priors and con-
 1246: tinuous robotic control, limitations present exciting avenues for future research.
 1247: Our current physical deployment relies exclusively on a single egocentric (ego-view) camera. While
 1248: this highlights the remarkable spatial reasoning capabilities of our video representations, single-
 1249: view setups are fundamentally susceptible to severe visual occlusions. In complex bimanual tasks,
 1250: the robot’s own arms or larger objects can temporarily block the camera’s line of sight, potentially
 1251: disrupting the temporal continuity of the visual features. Future iterations could explore the inte-
 1252: gration of auxiliary sensory inputs, such as wrist-mounted cameras or tactile feedback, fusing these
 1253: modalities with the video DiT backbone to maintain robust state estimation during severe occlusions.
 1254: Our real-world experiments achieved state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization using a pre-training
 1255: corpus that represents merely 15% of the data volume utilized by contemporary large-scale models
 1256: like GR00T. A natural and promising next step is to drastically scale the pre-training data across
 1257: diverse robotic embodiments (e.g., varying kinematics, grippers, and camera parameters). Given
 1258: the data-efficient nature of our dual flow-matching objective, scaling DiT4DiT with massive, cross-
 1259: embodiment datasets could yield a highly generalized robotic foundation model, further solidifying
 1260: video generation as the optimal scaling proxy for embodied intelligence.
