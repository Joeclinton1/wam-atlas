  546: 3.2   Training Procedure
  547: We adopt a two-stage training paradigm inspired by standard vision-language-action (VLA) manipulation frameworks,
  548: consisting of task-agnostic pretraining followed by task-specific adaptation.
  549: Pre-training. During the pretraining phase of the action model, we leverage the AgiBot-World-Beta dataset to specialize
  550: the pretrained visual-linguistic representations for action policy learning. The world model W is initialized with fixed
  551: parameters from GE-Base-LF to retain its spatiotemporal and semantic priors, while only the parameters of the action
  552: decoding module are updated. To reduce computational overhead, video generation is disabled during training. Instead,
  553: low-frame-rate visual memory sequences, consisting of four frames sampled at 5 Hz, are used as conditioning inputs,

  555: solely by ground-truth action trajectories, enabling the model to learn control-relevant dynamics entirely within the
  556: pretrained latent space. This process completes in approximately three days on a cluster of sixteen NVIDIA A100 GPUs.
  557: Task-specific adaptation tuning. To adapt the pretrained model for downstream robotic tasks, we employ a two-stage
  558: fine-tuning pipeline comprising video adaptation and action specialization, aimed at aligning general visual-linguistic
  559: representations with task-specific execution requirements. During the video adaptation phase, we update only the video
  560: generation components of the world model W, keeping the remaining parameters frozen. Fine-tuning is conducted on a
  561: composite dataset consisting of the full AgiBot-World corpus and a task-specific subset, with the latter upweighted by a
  562: factor of 10 to strengthen task alignment without sacrificing generalization. The sampling protocol is consistent with
  563: that used in GE-Base-LF to preserve temporal coherence. This phase is completed using 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs over

  571: 3.3   Asynchronous Inference
  572: To bridge the temporal gap between visual processing and motor control, we introduce the Slow-Fast Asynchronous
  573: Inference mode, which optimizes computational efficiency by exploiting asymmetries at two critical levels: denoising
  574: complexity and target frequency.
  575: Asymmetric Denoising Strategy. Our inference pipeline allocates computational resources based on the distinct
  576: requirements of each modality. The video DiT performs a single flow-matching denoising step per inference pass to
  577: generate visual latent tokens, which are then cached and reused throughout the action generation phase. The action
  578: model, requiring higher temporal resolution for precise control, executes five denoising steps, all conditioned on the
  579: same cached visual representations. This approach ensures that the forward pass for 54 steps is completed in 200ms on
  580: an onboard NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU mounted on a real-world robot, ensuring real-time inference capability.
  581: Beyond improving the denoising process, we exploit the inherent frequency mismatch between visual perception and
  582: motor control. The video DiT operates at 5 Hz, while the action model runs at 30 Hz, resulting in a temporal resolution
  583: ratio of 1 to 6. This decoupling enables sparse video prediction alongside dense action generation. By representing only
  584: selected future video frames, we significantly reduce the dimensionality of the video latent space, eliminating the need
  585: to process high-frequency visual sequences. This design allows the video DiT to operate efficiently in a compact latent
  586: space, while the action model retains the full temporal resolution necessary for accurate and responsive control.
  587: This dual-level optimization delivers substantial advantages for both training and deployment. During training, we
  588: eliminate the typical bottleneck caused by video loading and decoding by initializing hidden states with random Gaussian
  589: noise, optimizing the training process for large-scale video models. In deployment, the combination of single-step video
  590: denoising and reduced latent dimensionality enables efficient real-time operation on robotic hardware, facilitating the
  591: seamless integration of video generation and action execution.
