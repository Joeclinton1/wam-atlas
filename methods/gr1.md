  339: We follow the causal attention mechanism typically used in generative pre-trained models, except
  340: that all the [ACT] and [OBS] tokens are masked (Fig. 1). That is, during pre-training, a token
  341: can attend to all the tokens in the previous positions except the [OBS] tokens; during finetuning, a
  342: token can attend to all the tokens in the previous positions except the [ACT] and [OBS] tokens.
  343: We refer the reader to Radford et al. (2018) for more details about causal transformers.

  345: 3.2.3    O UTPUTS
  346: For video prediction, we attach a transformer decoder consisting of self-attention blocks and multi-
  347: layer perceptrons (MLPs). The decoder operates on the outputs corresponding the [OBS] tokens
  348: and mask tokens (Fig. 2(d)). Each mask token is a shared and learnable embedding added with a
  349: corresponding positional encoding. The output corresponding to a mask token reconstructs a patch
  350: of the predicted future image. Following He et al. (2022), the loss function Lvideo computes the
  351: mean squared error (MSE) between the reconstructed and original images in the pixel space. The
  352: outputs from the [ACT] tokens are passed through linear layers to predict the arm and gripper
  353: actions (Fig. 2(e)). Since the arm action is continuous, we use Smooth-L1 loss Larm for training.
  354: Gripper actions are optimized using Binary Cross Entropy (BCE) loss Lgripper .

  959: A.4   A BLATION S TUDIES
  960: 
  961: Video Prediction & Pre-training. In this section, we perform ablation studies to study how video
  962: generative pre-training helps GR-1 on learning visual robot manipulation. We investigate two vari-
  963: ants of GR-1. The first variant, denoted as GR-1 w/o Video Prediction & Pre-training, is trained
  964: from scratch and does not predict videos. That is, [OBS] tokens are removed from the input token
  965: sequence. The second variant, denoted as GR-1 w/o Video Pre-training, is trained from scratch and
  966: retains video prediction. On CALVIN benchmark, we perform experiments on ABCD→D split,
  967: ABC→D split, and 10% training data (Fig. 7(a) and Tab. 4). We also perform real-robot experi-
  968: ments in a pick-and-place setting which uses the training scene in Fig. 5. The robot is trained to
  969: perform picking an object on the desk/plate and placing it onto the plate/desk. Results are shown
  970: in Fig. 7(b). GR-1 outperforms both variants in all experiments. We hypothesize that this is be-
  971: cause the large-scale video pre-training helps GR-1 learn an accurate video prediction model which
  972: helps the robot understand what shall happen in future steps given the language instruction and
  973: previous observations. And this information acts as a strong signpost for the robot to generate per-
  974: tinent actions for rolling out trajectories. Without pre-training, the video prediction of GR-1 w/o
  975: Video Pre-training may not be as robust. On CALVIN benchmark, it outperforms GR-1 w/o Video
  976: Prediction & Pre-training but achieves a lower success rate in real robot experiments.
  977: Different Future Predictions. We compare the effectiveness of predicting images at different future
  978: steps (i.e. 1, 3, and 5) on CALVIN benchmark. Results are shown in Tab. 5. Pre-training is not used
  979: in this ablation. It is observed that increasing the step from 1 to 3 improves success rates. This may
